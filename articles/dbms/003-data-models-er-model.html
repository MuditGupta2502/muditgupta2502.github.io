Data Models & Entity-Relationship (ER) Model

Database Design | 17 min read

Why Data Modeling Comes Before Tables

Good databases are not built by starting with random SQL tables. They are built by understanding the domain first. Before implementation, we need a model of the real world: what objects exist, what facts we need to store about them, and how they are related. That is the role of data modeling.

A data model helps bridge the gap between business language and database structure. It lets stakeholders say, "a student can enroll in many courses" or "an employee belongs to one department," before anyone writes CREATE TABLE.

What Is a Data Model?

A data model is a formal way to describe data, relationships, constraints, and semantics. It provides a vocabulary for database design and prevents important rules from staying vague.

Three Common Levels of Modeling

  • Conceptual model: High-level business view, often represented using ER diagrams.
  • Logical model: More detailed structure that is closer to the target database style.
  • Physical model: Exact implementation with data types, indexes, and storage decisions.

Major Types of Data Models

  • Hierarchical model: Tree-like parent-child structure. Good for strictly nested relationships, but inflexible.
  • Network model: More flexible than hierarchical, but complex to manage.
  • Relational model: Data stored in tables with rows and columns. Still the dominant model for transactional systems.
  • Entity-Relationship model: Conceptual design model used before relational implementation.
  • NoSQL models: Document, key-value, column-family, and graph models optimized for different workloads.

What Makes the ER Model Important?

The ER model is popular because it is intuitive. It reflects how humans naturally think about systems: there are objects, those objects have properties, and objects relate to each other. Instead of starting with implementation details, ER modeling starts with meaning.

  • It is technology-independent.
  • It improves communication with non-technical stakeholders.
  • It exposes missing requirements early.
  • It reduces bad schema design choices later.

Core Components of the ER Model

Entity

An entity is a distinguishable real-world object or concept that we need to store data about. Examples include Student, Course, Department, Customer, Invoice, and Product.

  • Strong entity: Exists independently and has its own key.
  • Weak entity: Depends on another entity for identification or existence.

Example: An OrderItem may depend on an Order. It makes little sense without the order it belongs to.

Attribute

Attributes are properties of entities or relationships.

  • Simple attribute: Cannot be meaningfully divided, such as age.
  • Composite attribute: Can be split into parts, such as address into city, state, and postal code.
  • Single-valued attribute: One value per entity instance, such as date of birth.
  • Multi-valued attribute: Multiple values per instance, such as phone numbers.
  • Derived attribute: Computed from another value, such as age derived from date of birth.
  • Key attribute: Helps uniquely identify entity instances.

Relationship

A relationship captures an association between entities. Examples include Student enrolls in Course, Employee works in Department, and Customer places Order.

Relationships may also have attributes. For example, the relationship between Student and Course can have attributes like enrollment_date, grade, or semester.

Entity Set vs Entity Instance

An entity set is the collection of all entities of a type. A specific entity instance is one actual member of that set. 

Entity set: Student
Entity instances:
- Student(101, "Mudit")
- Student(102, "Aisha")

Relationship Degree

  • Unary relationship: Entity related to itself, such as Employee manages Employee.
  • Binary relationship: Two entity sets participate. This is the most common case.
  • Ternary relationship: Three entity sets participate, such as Supplier supplies Product to Warehouse.

Ternary relationships should not automatically be broken into separate binaries unless the semantics remain correct. Sometimes the relationship only makes sense when all three participants are considered together.

Cardinality Ratios

Cardinality tells us how many instances of one entity can be associated with instances of another.

  • One-to-One: One person has one passport.
  • One-to-Many: One department has many employees.
  • Many-to-One: Many employees belong to one department.
  • Many-to-Many: Many students enroll in many courses.

Many-to-many relationships are especially important because they usually become separate junction tables in the relational model.

Participation Constraints

Participation describes whether entity participation in a relationship is mandatory or optional.

  • Total participation: Every entity instance must participate in the relationship.
  • Partial participation: Participation is optional.

Example: Every bank account must belong to some customer, so Account has total participation in the ownership relationship. But not every customer must currently own an account, so Customer may have partial participation.

Weak Entities and Identifying Relationships

A weak entity cannot be uniquely identified by its own attributes alone. It depends on an owner entity and usually has a partial key. 

Employee(emp_id, name)
Dependent(dep_name, relation)

Dependent is weak because "dep_name" may not be unique globally.
It may be identified by (emp_id, dep_name).

Weak entities are useful when existence is naturally subordinate, not when the designer simply forgot to create a primary key.

Extended ER Model (EER)

Real domains often contain inheritance-like structure. The Extended ER model adds richer constructs for those cases.

Generalization and Specialization

Generalization combines specific entity sets into a broader supertype. Specialization does the reverse by breaking a general entity into subtypes. 

Person
|- Student
|- Teacher

Here, Student and Teacher inherit common attributes like name and date_of_birth from Person.

Disjointness and Completeness Constraints

  • Disjoint specialization: An entity can belong to only one subtype.
  • Overlapping specialization: An entity can belong to multiple subtypes.
  • Total specialization: Every supertype instance must belong to at least one subtype.
  • Partial specialization: Some supertype instances may belong to none of the subtypes.

How to Build a Good ER Diagram

  1. Identify the main business objects.
  2. List the facts you must store about each one.
  3. Mark which attributes uniquely identify instances.
  4. Define relationships and their cardinalities.
  5. Mark mandatory versus optional participation.
  6. Check for multi-valued or derived attributes.
  7. Validate the model against real-world business rules.

Common ER Modeling Mistakes

  • Treating attributes as entities without reason
  • Ignoring many-to-many relationships until implementation time
  • Forgetting participation constraints
  • Mixing business meaning with storage details too early
  • Using vague relationship names like "has" everywhere

Relationship names should express meaning clearly: enrolls in, teaches, belongs to, depends on.

ER to Relational Mapping

After conceptual modeling, we translate the ER design into relational tables.

  • Each strong entity becomes a table.
  • Composite attributes are broken into component columns.
  • Multi-valued attributes usually become separate tables.
  • One-to-many relationships are implemented with a foreign key on the many side.
  • Many-to-many relationships become junction tables.
  • Weak entities include the key of their owner entity.
Student(student_id, name)
Course(course_id, course_name)
Enrollment(student_id, course_id, semester, grade)

Summary

Data models help us think clearly before implementation, and the ER model is the most accessible starting point for that process. Entities, attributes, relationships, cardinality, participation, weak entities, and EER concepts together form the language of conceptual database design. If you can model the domain correctly at this stage, the later relational schema will be much cleaner.
